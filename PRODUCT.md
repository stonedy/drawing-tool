# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Designers and artists who want a lightweight creative space for drawing with visible process, motion, and line-by-line control. They are not trying to complete production illustration work in a full professional suite; they are exploring gesture, rhythm, gradients, replay, and visual behavior as part of an artistic experiment.

## Product Purpose

This product is an experimental art-making tool where every stroke remains alive after it is drawn. Users can replay, reverse, loop, hide, speed up, delete, and globally orchestrate drawn lines, turning a static sketch into a process-based visual composition. Success means the interface feels precise enough for designers, expressive enough for artists, and distinct from a generic whiteboard.

## Brand Personality

Experimental, refined, gallery-like.

The product should feel like a calm digital studio for kinetic line work: minimal enough to stay out of the artist's way, but carefully composed so the act of controlling strokes feels intentional and artful.

## Anti-references

This should not feel like a generic whiteboard, a basic browser paint demo, or a dull utility panel with no visual identity. It should avoid bland office-tool aesthetics, childish toy-like drawing UI, and Photoshop-level complexity. The reference direction is closer to the composed, design-led restraint of Units, especially its confident spacing, editorial calm, and polished art-direction, adapted for a functional creative tool rather than a marketing page.

## Design Principles

1. Keep the stroke as the hero: every control and layout decision should reinforce the idea that drawn lines are living, replayable objects.
2. Make control feel composed: dense tools are acceptable, but spacing, grouping, and hierarchy must feel deliberate rather than administrative.
3. Preserve artistic flow: drawing should stay immediate, with advanced controls revealed progressively instead of crowding the canvas.
4. Treat motion as material: playback, reverse, loop, and gradients are not effects bolted on later; they are the product's core expressive medium.
5. Prefer gallery-like restraint over decoration: the interface can be distinctive, but it should earn attention through proportion, rhythm, and precise interaction rather than visual noise.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Meet baseline accessibility expectations: clear text contrast, keyboard-operable controls where practical, visible focus states, reduced-motion support for non-essential interface animations, and no reliance on color alone to communicate critical state. The drawing canvas can remain pointer-first, but surrounding controls should remain understandable and usable without fine visual interpretation.
